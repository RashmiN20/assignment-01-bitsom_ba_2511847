## Database Recommendation

When designing a patient management system for a healthcare startup, the choice between **MySQL** (a relational database) and **MongoDB** (a NoSQL document store) hinges on the trade‑offs between **ACID and BASE principles**, as well as the **CAP theorem**. Healthcare data is highly sensitive, requiring strict consistency, durability, and transactional integrity. MySQL, with its ACID compliance, ensures that patient records, prescriptions, and billing information remain accurate and reliable even under concurrent access. This is critical in healthcare, where a single inconsistency could lead to medical errors or compliance violations. MySQL also benefits from mature tooling for schema enforcement, which aligns well with regulatory requirements such as **HIPAA**.

**MongoDB**, on the other hand, offers flexibility and scalability under the BASE model. It excels at handling unstructured or semi‑structured data, such as medical notes, sensor data, or patient‑generated health records. Its schema‑less design allows rapid iteration, which is attractive for startups experimenting with new features. However, MongoDB prioritizes availability and partition tolerance over strict consistency, which may introduce risks in core patient management functions where accuracy is paramount. In CAP theorem terms, **MySQL is typically CA (Consistency + Availability)**, while **MongoDB is often AP (Availability + Partition Tolerance)**. This distinction highlights why MySQL is better suited for critical healthcare records, while MongoDB shines in distributed, high‑volume analytical workloads.

Therefore, for the patient management system, MySQL is the stronger recommendation because consistency and reliability outweigh flexibility. However, if the startup later integrates a fraud detection module, the answer shifts slightly. Fraud detection often involves analyzing large volumes of heterogeneous data, logs, and behavioral patterns in near real‑time. MongoDB’s scalability and ability to ingest diverse datasets make it well‑suited for this analytical workload. In practice, a hybrid approach could be optimal: MySQL for core patient records and MongoDB for fraud detection and analytics. This combination leverages the strengths of both systems—ensuring patient safety while enabling innovation in fraud prevention.
